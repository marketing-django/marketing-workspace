# Quality assurance

## Guidelines for marketing strategy

A good product marketing strategy gives contributors a clear, shared answer to _who we're reaching_ (audiences), _what we're saying_ (brand voice, positioning, messaging), and _how we'll know it's working_ (KPIs, OKRs). It provides clear support for tactical work so limited volunteer effort goes much further. For example for campaigns (releases, surveys, elections), events, etc.

In this workspace, a strong marketing strategy follows those guidelines:

- **Is based on a clear methodology**. We build trust into our work by using explainable and documented ways to collaboratively create the strategy.
- **Starts with evidence, not opinion**. Claims about audiences, competitors, or channels are backed by research you can trace. With sources. Information that is un-sourced is marked explicitly.
- **Names specific audiences and their jobs to be done**. Vague audiences produce vague messaging. A good strategy tells you which personas matter and what they're trying to accomplish, so contributors can choose channels and write copy accordingly.
- **Derives positioning from real differentiators**. The strategy identifies what Django _already is_ and frames that distinctively against the landscape. It doesn't invent attributes Django ought to have.
- **Defines measurable outcomes**. Strategic goals translate into OKRs or equivalent metrics that are concrete enough to report on and revisit. A contributor reading the strategy should understand what success looks like and how progress is tracked.

### Focused and intentional

- **Constraints the path ahead**. A good strategy should make it clear that there are things we _won’t_ do, rather than leaving everything open and making everything a priority.
- **Takes a stance**. The strategy makes trade-offs explicit. Conceding where Django can't win and claiming where it can. It answers _why Django?_ in a way competitors can't copy, and it says no to directions that dilute that answer.
- **Is self-aware about its own limits**. Drafts are marked as drafts. Open questions are called out rather than glossed over. When the strategy depends on governance decisions, budget, or working group capacity that doesn't yet exist, it says so.

### Things to avoid

- **Wishlist positioning**. Don't build marketing strategy around features or attributes you wish Django had. Marketing can inform Django’s product direction but it should never set it.
- **Vanity metrics**. Traffic, stars, followers. These look good in a deck and may be ok interim proxies, but they don't tell you whether the strategy is working. Tie every metric back to a strategic outcome: awareness among non-adopters, sponsorship conversion, contributor retention.
- **Strategy-by-committee**. A good strategy has a point of view. The strategy should make someone nod _and_ make someone else raise eyebrows. That's how we know it's actually saying something useful.
- **Assuming the audience already cares**. Django has 20 years of goodwill within its community, but outside that bubble, most developers are unconvinced.
- **Premature polish**. A draft that's never shared because it's "not ready yet" is worse than a rough draft that gets feedback. Mark work in progress honestly, publish early, and iterate. The strategy is living content, not a one-shot deliverable.
